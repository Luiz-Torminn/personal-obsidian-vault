# Business Strategy

## Definition
A business strategy is a comprehensive plan focused on analyzing the external environment and determining the optimal allocation of financial resources, human capital, and capacity. Its ultimate goal is to differentiate the enterprise and create an exploitable advantage.

---

## Competitive Advantage

### What it is
A group of characteristics that differentiates an enterprise or product, making it unique and difficult for competitors to replicate within a market ecosystem.

### Identification & Emergence
Advantage is rooted in **differences**—specifically, performing a different set of activities to deliver a unique mix of value compared to rivals. 

According to Michael Porter, these positions emerge from three sources:
- **Variety-based Positioning:** Choosing a narrow subset of product/service offerings rather than a broad range. Success comes from delivering these specific offerings faster, better, or at a lower cost.
- **Need-based Positioning:** Aiming to serve most or all needs of a specific customer segment (e.g., price-sensitive customers or those requiring high customization).
- **Access-based Positioning:** Segmenting customers based on how they are accessed (e.g., geography or scale), such as locating stores exclusively in low-income neighborhoods.

> [!NOTE]
> These positions are not mutually exclusive; they often overlap in a successful strategy.

### Core Strategic Inquiry
> "What is your strategy for gaining competitive advantage? Will it differentiate your company in ways that attract customers from rivals? Will it draw new customers into the market? What value does your strategy aim to provide? Can it deliver?"

---

## The 6-Step Formulation Process

### Step 1: External Analysis (Threats & Opportunities)
Analyze the economic environment and shifting trends to find profitable actions and their associated risks.
> "What is the economic environment in which we must operate? How is it changing? What opportunities are there for profitable action? What are the risks associated with these opportunities?"

### Step 2: Internal Analysis (Resources & Capabilities)
A strategy only succeeds if it is backed by the right people and resources.
> "What are our competencies as an organization? How do these give us an advantage over our competitors? Which resources support or constrain our actions?"

### Step 3: Strategy Development
Develop multiple alternatives and vet them thoroughly.
- Create many alternatives (combine the best parts of different ideas).
- Check all facts and question every assumption.
- Identify and fill information gaps.
- Vet choices with trusted advisors to avoid **groupthink**.

### Step 4: Build Activity Fit
Combine activities into a mutually supporting chain. This "linkage" makes the strategy difficult for competitors to imitate because they would have to replicate the entire system, not just one part.

### Step 5: Create Alignment
Strategy is only effective if the organization moves in unison.
1. **People:** Every employee must understand the strategy and their specific role in it.
2. **Resources:** Marketing, compensation, and physical assets must be deployed in direct support of the strategic goals.

### Step 6: Implementation Readiness
A powerful strategy is impotent without effective execution. Preparation for implementation is as critical as the design of the strategy itself.

---

## Specialized Case: SaaS & The Internet Economy

In the internet economy, the driving force is **demand-side economies of scale** (Network Effects).

- **Network Effects:** As the platform grows, the value per transaction increases.
- **Data Flywheel:** Larger networks generate richer data, leading to better matches between supply and demand.
- **Scale:** Greater scale attracts more participants, creating a cycle that can lead to market monopolies.

---

## Strategic Agility & Resilience

Success is rarely a straight line. Because every startup is an experiment, failure is a possibility that must be managed.

- **The Pivot:** Recognize bad news quickly and respond with a revised strategy.
- **Incrementalism:** If something works, do more of it.
- **Niche Expansion:** Master a small, niche market first, then use those learnings to enter subsequent niches, altering the product as necessary.

> "Successful entrepreneurs are adept at both these capabilities. They are also masters of incrementalism—that is, if they find that something is working, they do more of it."

# Business Model

## Definition
A business model is a foundational document that helps the entrepreneur—and potential investors—understand the operational logic of the business. It explains what the company does and how its key components integrate to create and capture value.

---

## Core Components

### 1. Market Analysis
To understand the economic viability of the model, four key areas must be defined:

- **Revenue Sources:** Identifying the different branches of the business where money originates (e.g., B2C, B2B, advertising, service fees, or subscriptions).
- **Cost Drivers:** Distinguishing between fixed costs (required to run the company regardless of volume) and variable costs (which fluctuate based on production or sales volume).
- **Investment Size:** The measurable level of capital required to launch the enterprise and sustain operations until it becomes self-sufficient.
- **Critical Success Factors (CSFs):** The essential elements that must happen for the business to succeed. 
> [!QUOTE] 
> "These might be the ability to roll out new products on a sustained basis, success in reaching some critical mass of business within a certain time, and so on."

### 2. Delivery & Discovery
This section outlines how the product or service reaches the market.

**The Discovery Plan**
A strategic plan to attract your target customers. To execute this effectively:
- **Customer Insight:** You must know your customers deeply enough to understand where and how to reach them.
- **Creative Outreach:** To remain competitive, you must look beyond saturated channels like Instagram or Facebook and find ways to engage with customers directly.

---

# Business Plan

## Definition
A document that explains a business opportunity, identifies the target market, and details how the organization plans to pursue it.

A strong plan also describes the unique qualifications that you and your management team bring to the effort, explains the resources you will need, and forecasts financial results over a reasonable time horizon.

**Logic (at a glance):** Market $\rightarrow$ Opportunity $\rightarrow$ How/Why we will pursue it $\rightarrow$ What we need to get there

---

## Importance
- You are unlikely to secure serious funding (VCs, angels, banks) without a business plan.
- Helps you identify gaps, prioritize where to invest effort and money, seek targeted advice, and build an initial budget.

---

## Key Elements
A business plan should “show your work”: it must demonstrate professionalism, expertise, and trustworthiness—not just optimism.

- Keep the plan brief and clear.
- Move supporting details to the appendix.

![[Captura de Tela 2026-06-04 às 14.05.16.png]]

---

## Executive Summary
- Often the most important section for investors.
- Roughly two pages.
- A snapshot of the entire plan, written so an intelligent reader can understand the business in a few minutes.
- Consider including a one-sentence “impact pitch” summary.

---

## The Opportunity
Outline:
- The problem and its scope
- Market trends that may shape how the problem evolves over time
- The solution you propose

Help readers see and appreciate the opportunity you identified. Highlight:
- The economics underlying the problem
- The factors that will drive your solution’s success (e.g., market penetration, product innovation)

This is also a suitable place to state the magnitude of the funding you are seeking.

Maintain brevity and clarity.

---

## The Company, Its Offering, and Strategy
Expand on the proposed solution:
- The product or service
- The company and how it is organized
- The strategy to win in the market

Maintain objectivity and clarity.

What goes in:
- Goals and growth plan
- [[Business Model]]
- [[Business Strategy]]
- [[Proprietorships]]

---

## Team

### Current Team
Include short biographies that highlight each member’s relevant experience and qualifications, and explain why the team is capable of executing the mission.

**Questions your business plan should answer about the team:**
- Where are the founders and other key team members from?
- Where did they study?
- Where have they worked, and in what roles?
- What have they accomplished (professionally and personally)?
- What is their reputation within the business community?
- What aspects of their experience are directly relevant to the opportunity?
- What skills, abilities, and knowledge do they bring?
- How realistic are they about the venture’s chances and the challenges it will face?
- Who else needs to be on the team?
- Are they prepared to recruit high-quality people?
- How will they respond to adversity?
- Do they have the judgment to make the inevitable hard choices?
- How committed are they to this venture?
- What are their motivations?

A table listing key team members’ names, titles, and salaries can also be useful.

### Future Team
Describe how the team must evolve:
- How do you anticipate the team will need to grow?
- What capabilities will be required to execute the strategy?

---

## Marketing Plan
Marketing is often the activity most associated with success or failure.

A sound, realistic marketing plan provides the best assurance that the company will maintain a solid connection with its customers.

Be clear about:
- Who your customers (primary market) are
- Market size (number of potential customers and projected revenue)
- Requirements of customer segments (e.g., convenience, rapid delivery, customization)
- How you will access each segment (e.g., distributors, e-commerce, direct sales force)
- Sales and promotion approaches (e.g., social campaigns, content strategy, freemium, direct email)
- How customers make purchase decisions
- Customer price sensitivity
- Customer acquisition cost (CAC) and retention cost
- Competitors’ strengths/weaknesses and how they may react to your entry

> [!NOTE]
> All of these points should be supported by credible market intelligence.

---

## Operating Plan
An operating plan addresses the operational details of converting inputs into outputs customers value.

Key questions include:
- What supplier relationships do you have (or need)?
- How much inventory is required (if applicable)?
- Which day-to-day tasks are performed internally vs. outsourced?

This applies to manufacturing/distribution businesses as well as websites, apps, and platform businesses.

---

## Financial Plan
If the company is already operating, it should have core financial statements:
- **Balance Sheet:** what the company owns (assets) and how those assets are financed (liabilities and owners’ equity) at a given date.
- **Income Statement**
- **Cash-Flow Statement**

Include sales forecasts here as well, but ensure assumptions are realistic and explicitly justified.

# Proprietorships

## Sole Proprietorship
- Owned by a single individual.
- The business and the owner are legally the same entity.

### Advantages
- Complete control over the business.
- Direct control of finances (investment decisions and withdrawals).
- Simple and inexpensive to dissolve.

### Practical tips
- Separate household and business finances.
- Track business expenses in an organized system.
- If you are not using your legal name as the business name, you will likely need to file a **fictitious name** (also called a *DBA*, depending on jurisdiction).

### Disadvantages
- Less attractive to venture capital (VC) investors.
- Funding is limited by your personal financial capacity and credit.
- Lawsuits and debts apply directly to you (personal liability).

---

## General Partnership
- Owned by two or more partners.

### What should be agreed between partners
Partners should formally agree on:
- The amount and nature of each partner’s capital contributions (e.g., cash, intellectual property, equipment, or property).
- How profits and losses will be allocated.
- Salaries and draws against profits.
- Management responsibilities and decision-making authority.
- Consequences of withdrawal, retirement, disability, or death of a partner.
- The process for dissolution and liquidation of the partnership.

### Disadvantages
- Important decisions may take longer due to shared authority.
- Each partner may be personally liable for the actions and mistakes of the others (depending on jurisdiction and agreements).

## Limited Partnership (LP)
A partnership structure with at least one **general partner** and one or more **limited partners**.

- **General partner(s):** Manage the business and assume unlimited liability.
- **Limited partner(s):** Do not participate in management and are liable only up to their capital contribution (plus any additional obligations they explicitly accept).

### Reasons to use
- Allows a combination of operating expertise (general partners) and passive capital (limited partners).
- Profits and losses can be allocated differently among partners (subject to the partnership agreement and applicable law).

---

## Corporation
A corporation is a separate legal entity from its owners, typically owned through shares.

- Ownership is represented by shares, which may be privately held or publicly traded.
- Public corporations generally become publicly traded through an IPO; share prices can fluctuate thereafter.
- Oversight is typically provided by a **board of directors**, elected to represent shareholders.

# Proprietorships

## Sole Proprietorship
- Owned by one individual.
- The business and the owner are the same legal entity.

### Advantages
- Full control over decisions and operations.
- Direct control of finances (investment and withdrawals).
- Simple to dissolve.

### Practical tips
- Separate personal and business finances.
- Track expenses consistently.
- If you do not use your legal name as the business name, you may need to register a **fictitious name/DBA** (jurisdiction-dependent).

### Disadvantages
- Typically unattractive to VC investors.
- Capital is limited to personal funds and credit.
- Unlimited personal liability for debts and lawsuits.

---

## General Partnership
- Owned by two or more partners.

### What to agree on (in writing)
- Capital contributions (cash, IP, property, equipment, etc.).
- Profit and loss allocation.
- Salaries and draws.
- Roles, responsibilities, and decision rights.
- Terms for withdrawal, retirement, disability, or death.
- Dissolution and liquidation process.

### Disadvantages
- Slower decision-making due to shared authority.
- Potential joint liability for partners’ actions (depends on jurisdiction and agreements).

## Limited Partnership (LP)
A partnership with at least one **general partner** and one or more **limited partners**.

- **General partner(s):** Manage the business; unlimited liability.
- **Limited partner(s):** No management role; liability typically limited to their contribution (plus any obligations explicitly assumed).

### Why use it
- Combines operating expertise (general partners) with passive capital (limited partners).
- Allows flexible profit/loss allocations (subject to law and the partnership agreement).
